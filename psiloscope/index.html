Psiloscope

Collective Motion Visualization

2016–2017

The movements of one hundred dancers control a dynamic three-dimensional visualization, opening a new medium for collective interaction. Trance rituals transform group consciousness. It is thought that music provides a rhythmic driver that increases the coherence between individuals' conscious experience. This is maximized when the kinesthetic experience of one's own body is synchronized with both the music and the visual experience of others' movements.

Psytrance producers have used computers and audio technology to explore the power of sound to enhance these experiences and extend them to groups of thousands of people. The potential for visual manipulation to enhance collective conscious experience remains comparatively unexplored, largely because the necessary technology has not been available. Psiloscope seeks to answer the question: if the motion of each individual could be captured, enhanced and visualized, how could this new medium of feedback alter individual and collective experience?

Boom Festival, 2016

MindCrystal Prototype — Lux Frágil, Lisbon (2017)

Design Concept
Each of the one hundred sensors controls the position, movement, orientation, size and color of an icon or cursor on screen. The visualization is inspired by a multi-channel oscilloscope: sensor readings are recorded in sampled 'beats' flowing down the screen, revealing the recent history of motion. A moving camera enhances the impact of the dynamic three-dimensional scene.
Technology
Custom "Tuna" wireless motion sensors (2×3 cm microcontroller-based devices) with 9-channel sensory input (accelerometer, gyroscope, magnetometer) operating on a low-latency WiFi network. Video-based tracking system (MESh) provides simultaneous 3D localization. The Bonsai framework handles real-time sensor-to-visualization mapping, allowing flexible remapping and live parameter adjustment. Sensor autonomy: 90–120 minutes. System supports synchronized recording and streaming of up to 100 devices.
Collaborators
Zachary Mainen (concept, visual design, implementation), Gonçalo Lopes (visual design, software architecture, programming), João Frazão (visual design, programming and implementation), Filipe Carvalho (hardware and electronic design), Gil Costa (graphic design), Gonçalo Guiomar, Scott Rennie (site crew)
Context
Part of the Collective Motion Laboratory, Champalimaud Centre for the Unknown, Lisbon. First deployed at Boom Festival 2016 (Idanha-a-Nova, Portugal), then evolved and deployed as MindCrystal at Lux Frágil nightclub, Lisbon (2017). In 2024, the team returned to Boom Festival to conduct interviews with participants as part of Latent States Camp, revisiting the questions that Psiloscope first posed. The project represents a conviction that perception and collective experience could be studied not in spite of the festival context but because of it — bringing laboratory instruments into the place where experience actually happens.
