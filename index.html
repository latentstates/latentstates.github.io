What is it like to experience?

Yourself. The world. Other people. A machine.

Latent States explores the hidden worlds within us — through art, science, and the spaces where they meet.

LS · 2016–2026

Psiloscope began with a hundred wireless sensors on a dance floor at Boom festival. MindCrystal added EEG under trance conditions. The question: what does collective experience look like when you externalize it? We brought instruments designed for the laboratory into the place where experience actually happens — a festival, at night, among strangers, in motion.

Psiloscope (2016) · MindCrystal (2018) · Boom Festival, Portugal

At the Champalimaud Foundation warehouse, we built spaces where visitors encountered AI — not as tool or threat but as mirror. Atavic Forest was a generative neural network trained on 180,000 images, steered by collective movement in the room. Latent Space I invited you to sit at a terminal, talk to an AI, then enter a VR dream shaped by what you'd said. Polylith simulated deep connection to natural environments through body tracking and spatial sound. Each installation asked: what is it like when a system reflects your hidden states back at you?

Polylith (2022) · Atavic Forest (2022–23) · LS1 (2022–23) · Metamersion, Champalimaud Foundation

The Latent States Camp at Waking Life festival is a laboratory without walls. Not a lab outpost at a festival — a place where ideas transform into situated experience. We study perception where perception actually happens: 240 participants, experiments on seeing, hearing, tasting, time. How do psychedelics change what you see? Does the color of a cup change how things taste? Does the festival experience itself change you? Phenomenology — the careful description of lived experience — is our primary instrument. The question is not how perception works but what perception is, when you stop controlling the conditions.

Latent States Camp (2023–) · Waking Life Festival, Portugal

LS9 is an autonomous alien creature with eight articulated arms, thirteen channels of spatial audio, and its own behavioral states — curiosity, tracking, withdrawal, alarm. It watches you with infrared sensors and neural networks. It decides whether to approach or retreat. The observer becomes the observed. A decade of asking what it is like to experience arrives at its inversion: what is it like to be experienced?

LS9: Cephalopod Brain (2026)
