Human Open Field

2019 · Open-Air Experiment · Pavilhão do Conhecimento, Lisbon
EXPAND residence · Espaço do Tempo · Audiência Zero

The classic rodent open field test — a cornerstone of behavioural neuroscience since 1934 — reimagined for humans. Visitors explored an empty room wearing augmented reality goggles that distorted their visual perception, while EEG and movement tracking captured the neural and bodily traces of their experience.

The setup was elemental: an empty room at Pavilhão do Conhecimento, Lisbon’s science museum. Participants wore AR goggles fitted with four types of visual filters — monochrome with background subtraction, edge emphasis, live projection mapping with kaleidoscopic graphics. Wireless EEG was embedded directly in the goggles. Cameras tracked position, posture, and speed throughout each session.

The experiment was grounded in Jean-Luc Marion’s phenomenology of “givenness.” The AR distortions functioned as artistic frames that diverted attention, created contrast, invited exploration, and deepened perception — the four characteristics Marion attributes to artwork. Each filter became a different mode of encounter with the same bare space.

After each session, participants sat for phenomenological interviews: open-ended, non-standardised conversations about their experience. Not surveys, not checklists — genuine attempts to access lived experience. The gap between what the sensors measured and what participants reported became the experiment’s central object of study.
